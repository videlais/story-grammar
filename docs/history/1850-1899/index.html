Mechanical Verse, 1850–1899

Permutational print, the analytical engine, and the chance of the page

Combinatorial writing is far older than the computer. By the mid-nineteenth century European print culture had already inherited the "proteus" tradition of permutational verse — lines whose words could be reshuffled into thousands of metrically valid poems, a practice dating to Julius Caesar Scaliger and Georg Philipp Harsdörffer in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.[1] The 1850–1899 period is best understood as a hinge: the literary appetite for recombination meeting the first mathematical machines capable of executing rules.

The analytical engine as a generator

In her 1843 notes on Babbage's Analytical Engine, Ada Lovelace speculated that the machine "might compose elaborate and scientific pieces of music of any degree of complexity," provided the rules of harmony could be expressed numerically.[2] This is the earliest clear statement that a symbol-manipulating machine could generate cultural artifacts — the conceptual seed of every later grammar engine.

The book as combinatorial object

At the close of the century, Stéphane Mallarmé's Un coup de dés jamais n'abolira le hasard (1897) dispersed words across the page so that reading order, and thus meaning, became partly chance-governed.[3] Mallarmé's unfinished Livre imagined a book whose pages could be permuted into many readings — a print prefiguration of the recombinant text.[4]

"All thought emits a throw of the dice." — Mallarmé, 1897

Florian Cramer locates these experiments in a continuous "combinatory imagination" running from medieval lullism to modern code, in which language is treated as a finite set of parts awaiting recombination.[1]
